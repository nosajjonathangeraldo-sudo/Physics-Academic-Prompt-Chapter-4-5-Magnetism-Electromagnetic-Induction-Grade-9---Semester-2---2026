Magnetism

Magnetic Poles

Every magnet has two ends called the North and South poles, where the magnetic force is strongest. Opposite poles attract each other, while similar poles push away. Even when a magnet is split, each piece still has both poles.

The Earth is also a giant magnet, with its own North and South magnetic poles. This is why a compass works, as its needle aligns with the Earth’s magnetic field.

Attraction and Repulsion

Magnets can either pull or push each other. Different poles attract, while the same poles repel.

Magnetic Materials

Some materials are strongly affected by magnets. Examples include iron, nickel, cobalt, and certain metal mixtures.

Magnetization and Demagnetization

Magnetization can be done by:

- Rubbing with a magnet
- Using electric current
- Hammering in a magnetic field

Demagnetization can be done by:

- Heating to a high temperature
- Striking or dropping the object
- Applying alternating current

Magnetic Field and Right-Hand Rule

A wire carrying current creates a circular magnetic field around it. The right-hand rule helps determine the direction: point your thumb in the direction of the current, and your fingers show the direction of the field.

Electromagnetism and Uses

Electromagnets are created using electric current. They are used in devices such as motors, transformers, MRI machines, and cranes for lifting heavy metal objects.
